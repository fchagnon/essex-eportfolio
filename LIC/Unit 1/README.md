# Unit 1: What is Computing?

## Unit Topic

This unit introduces computing as a field, its major Bodies of Knowledge
(Software Engineering, Data Science, Cyber Security, Information
Management, Enterprise IT, and Artificial Intelligence), and how
computing technologies drive business transformation, alongside an
introduction to Equality, Diversity, and Inclusion considerations in
computing.

## Learning Outcomes

On completion of this unit, I should be able to:

- Identify and critically analyse computing challenges and processes in
  business systems.
- Gather and synthesise information from multiple sources to understand
  computing concepts.
- Articulate the legal, social, ethical, and professional considerations
  in computing.

## Formative Assignment

A case study discussion on AI-powered business assistants (ChatGPT,
Google Bard, Microsoft Copilot), using PwC's internal knowledge
management adoption and Amazon's logistics optimisation as examples,
addressing three discussion points: how AI-powered automation transforms
businesses, what ethical concerns arise from AI adoption, and how
different computing disciplines contribute to AI-powered assistants.

Full post: https://github.com/fchagnon/essex-eportfolio/tree/main/LIC/Unit%201

## Reflection

**Identifying and analysing computing challenges in business systems.**
My original post framed the shift to AI-powered assistants as a move
from deterministic execution to probabilistic inference: traditional
automation (RPA) reduces toil through rigid, pre-programmed rules but is
brittle outside its design scope, while AI shifts humans from
"programmers of logic" to "evaluators of intent" (Ahuja, 2024). The
underlying business change isn't task automation, it's a shift in what
humans are actually responsible for managing.

**Gathering and synthesising information from multiple sources.** Paul
Sellers's response to the same case study identified something my own
post hadn't addressed at all, the case study itself provides almost no
supporting evidence for its own claims about PwC or Amazon, asserting
outcomes without demonstrating them. That's a genuinely different, more
skeptical reading of the same material than the one I brought, and a
useful check against taking a case study's framing at face value simply
because it's presented as an example.

**Articulating ethical and professional considerations.** My own post
raised AI governance and the risk of hallucination or amplified bias
requiring a "human-in-the-loop" partnership (Ahuja, 2024). Replying to
Paul, I brought in a different angle entirely, Brandon Sanderson's
keynote argument that the value of creative work lies in the process of
creation, not the output, and that handing that process to AI risks
producing polished results while never developing the underlying skill
or judgment that made the process valuable in the first place (Sanderson,
2026). Applied beyond art specifically, this is a genuine, if less
commonly discussed, professional consideration for computing more
broadly: automating a skill away doesn't just remove labor, it can
remove the developmental process that produces expertise in the people
who would otherwise have done it.
